What we love about Metroidvanias can be said in only one way. We seek an adventure in a world so interconnected that simply changing one thing in a subsequent playthrough can change the entire game experience.

What is a Metroidvania? A Metroidvania is a coined term combining the names of Metroid and Castlevania. This sub-genre of Action-adventure game is designed with a focus on non-linearity and utility-gated exploration. The term was popularized by Jeremy Parish, who tries to catalogue all Metroidvanias on his website, retronauts.com. While Metroidvanias are almost considered a niche sub-genre, their ideas and designs are very prevalent in most games.

Metroidvanias are heavily based around a player's ability to memorize the world and abilities that they have; And to use them in clever ways to explore and unearth areas/items that were previously unreachable. For many people a single playthrough is all that is needed, but Metroidvanias gain a lot of their popularity from 'sequence breaks'. A sequence break is when a player uses in-game knowledge and skill to perform tricks that yield items and gear out of order from the intended gameplay. This kind of gameplay makes designing a Metroidvania very difficult and sometimes frustrating. One popular Metroidvania is called Ori and the blind forest. Thomas Mahler of Moon Studios, who developed the game, stated, “players remembering the levels is part of the core design.” This means that the world that developers of Metroidvanias must design unique areas across the entire game to make it easier for players to remember. Similarly, Daniel Smith of Moon Studios who was the executive producer over Ori's sequel, Ori and the Will of the Wisps, stated, “I don't think people generally consider how difficult it is to make a Metroidvania game. Everything is so interconnected that if you change one aspect of the game, it's just inevitable that it's going to influence the rest.”
